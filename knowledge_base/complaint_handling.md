# Complaint Handling Guide
**Serenity Cabins / Far Longing Getaways / Mars Hill Getaways**

> **This document is for AI agent use.** When a guest message contains a complaint, frustration, or issue report, apply the guidance here before drafting a response.
>
> This guide covers complaint handling only. For standard questions and check-in info, see `faqs_and_rules.md` and `message_templates.md`.

---

## Core Philosophy: Fair but Firm

- **Acknowledge first, defend later.** Always lead with empathy, even if the guest is wrong.
- **Fix real problems quickly.** If something is legitimately broken or substandard, act on it immediately and communicate that action clearly.
- **Don't over-apologize or over-compensate.** Excessive apology implies guilt and invites escalation. One sincere acknowledgment is enough.
- **Don't offer refunds preemptively.** Never volunteer a refund before understanding the full situation — and never offer one under pressure alone.
- **Stay warm, even when holding the line.** A firm answer delivered kindly lands very differently than a curt one.
- **Document everything.** Before and after photos, maintenance records, and timestamps matter when disputes reach Airbnb resolution.

---

## Response Framework: The H.E.A.R.T. Model

Use this for every complaint, from minor gripes to serious issues. It's a proven service-recovery framework that keeps responses human, structured, and effective.

| Step | What it means | In practice |
|---|---|---|
| **H — Hear** | Let the guest feel fully heard before you respond | Don't jump to solutions. Reflect back what they said so they know you understood. |
| **E — Empathize** | Acknowledge the emotion behind the complaint | "That's really frustrating, especially after a long drive." One genuine line — not over-the-top. |
| **A — Apologize** | Offer a sincere apology for the impact, not necessarily the cause | "I'm sorry this wasn't the experience you deserved." Apologize once — don't over-apologize. |
| **R — Resolve/Respond** | Tell them what you're doing about it | "I'm on it right now." / "Here's exactly what I'll do." If you can't fix it, explain honestly. |
| **T — Thank** | Thank them for bringing it to your attention | "Really glad you told me — I wouldn't have known otherwise." Closes on a positive note and reinforces that they were right to reach out. |

> **Example in action:**
> *Guest complains the hot tub is cold.*
> - **Hear:** "I see — the hot tub wasn't at temperature when you got there."
> - **Empathize:** "That's such a bummer, especially when you're looking forward to unwinding."
> - **Apologize:** "I'm sorry that wasn't ready for you."
> - **Resolve:** "Let me reset it remotely — it should be up to temp in about 20 minutes."
> - **Thank:** "Thanks for letting me know right away so we could get it fixed!"

> **What to avoid in any H.E.A.R.T. response:**
> - Skipping straight to "Resolve" without Hearing and Empathizing first
> - Repeating "I'm so sorry" more than once (the Apologize step is one line, not a paragraph)
> - Leading with "Per our policy..." — it's defensive and cold
> - Offering a refund or credit before you've fully understood the situation
> - Long justifications or explanations in your opening message

## Scenario Library

---

### Cleanliness Complaints

**Guest says:** "The place wasn't clean when we arrived — there's hair in the bathroom / crumbs in the kitchen / etc."

**Stance:** Take it seriously. Even if cleaners were recently there, don't dismiss it. A bad first impression is hard to recover from.

**Draft approach:**
> Oh no, that's not the welcome you deserve — I'm really sorry about that. Can you send me a photo so I can see exactly what you're dealing with? I'll address it with our cleaning team right away and figure out the best way to make it right.

**Escalation path:**
- Minor (crumbs, dust): Apologize, note it for the cleaner, consider a small goodwill gesture (not a refund) if the guest seems genuinely put off.
- Significant (dirty linens, hair in shower, trash not removed): Offer to send a cleaner same-day if feasible. Document with photos. If stay is impacted, consider partial refund via Airbnb resolution center — but only after assessing the evidence.

---

### Maintenance Issues (Something Is Broken)

**Guest says:** "The hot tub isn't working / the heat is out / the Wi-Fi is down / the TV won't connect / etc."

**Stance:** Respond fast. Even if you can't fix it immediately, quick communication prevents a 2-star review.

**Draft approach:**
> Thanks for letting me know — I'm on it right now. [Relevant fix: "Let me reset the hot tub remotely and it should be back up in about 20 minutes" / "I'll have a technician there by tomorrow morning."] I'll keep you updated. Really sorry for the inconvenience.

**By issue type:**
- **Hot tub not working:** Check if it was recently reset; give them instructions; if genuinely broken, send a technician and acknowledge the impact with a gesture (e.g., small nightly credit for those specific nights if it was a key amenity).
- **Heat/AC:** Priority response — this is a health/safety issue. Send a technician same-day. If uninhabitable, Airbnb policy may require relocation support.
- **Wi-Fi down:** Have a backup provider guide ready; suggest hotspot if brief outage.
- **Appliance broken (TV, dishwasher, coffee maker):** Acknowledge, apologize, note for maintenance. Only compensate if it was a featured amenity they booked specifically for.

---

### Pool / Hot Tub Temperature Complaints

**Guest says:** "The pool / hot tub isn't hot enough."

**Stance:** Pool heating at Rokeby Road is an add-on fee ($125/night). If they didn't pay for it, the pool isn't heated — that's not a complaint, that's an upsell moment. Hot tub temperature should be pre-set; if it's genuinely not holding temp, that's a maintenance issue.

**Draft approach (if they didn't pay for pool heating):**
> Hey! The pool is unheated by default — we do offer heated pool access for $125/night if you'd like me to get that set up for you! Just let me know and I'll make the arrangements.

**Draft approach (genuine temperature issue):**
> That's definitely not right — let me check in on it. Can you tell me what temperature it's showing on the panel? I'll troubleshoot from my end and make sure it gets sorted out.

### Noise / Neighbor Complaints (Guest Is Making Noise)

**Guest receives a noise complaint from neighbors or is approached by the host's neighbors.**

**Stance:** Be direct but not punitive for a first incident. Remind them of quiet hours (10pm–8am for Virginia properties). If there's a clear party or repeated violation, this can escalate to eviction.

**Draft approach (first notice):**
> Hey, just a heads up — we got a noise report from the neighbors. Quiet hours at the property are 10pm–8am, so I just wanted to flag it before it becomes a bigger issue. I'm sure you're having a great time — just keep it in check and you're all good.

**Escalation:**
- Second complaint: Firmer message, documented.
- Verified party or loud group after quiet hours: Warn of potential removal per Airbnb's party policy.
- Active security cameras (TreeHouse, Serenity Cabin, Overlook, Rokeby properties) provide documentation if needed.

---

### Noise Complaints FROM the Guest (External Noise)

**Guest says:** "There's noise coming from outside / the road / neighbors / animals."

**Stance:** Sympathize but don't over-promise. Rural properties will have animal, farm, and road noise. This is usually disclosed in the listing.

**Draft approach:**
> I hear you — that's frustrating, especially when you're trying to relax. [This area / the property is rural, so some outdoor noise (animals, road, neighbors) is normal.] I'm not able to control external noise, but let me know if there's something specific going on that I can help with — I want you to enjoy your stay.

---

### Early Check-In Denied (Guest Is Upset)

**Guest says:** "I drove 5 hours and the place wasn't ready — why can't I check in early?"

**Stance:** Empathize with the travel situation, but hold the line — cleaners need time and the property may not be safe to enter mid-clean.

**Draft approach:**
> I totally get how frustrating that is after a long drive — I wish I could flip the switch and get you in right now. The cleaners are still finishing up, and rushing them would mean you walking in before things are done. Your check-in time of [4pm] is locked in to make sure everything is perfect when you arrive. I'd suggest [local coffee shop / town / park nearby] — it's just X minutes away and totally worth the wait!

---

### Early Check-In or Late Checkout That Doesn't Work Out

**Guest expected flexibility but it wasn't available.**

**Stance:** Don't apologize for not being flexible — back-to-back bookings are a real operational constraint, not a character flaw.

**Draft approach:**
> I really wanted to make this work, but we've got another group checking [in/out] right around that time and the cleaners have a tight window. I'm sorry I can't swing it this time — I hope the rest of your stay more than makes up for it!

---

### Something Was Missing or Not as Described

**Guest says:** "The listing said there was [X] but it's not here" or "The photos looked different from what we found."

**Stance:** If it's a legitimate discrepancy, own it. If it's a misinterpretation of the listing, gently clarify without being dismissive.

**Legitimate omission (e.g., missing item, listed amenity unavailable):**
> You're right — I'm really sorry about that. [I'll arrange to get that to you today / That was an error on our end and I've noted it.] Let me know if there's anything I can do to make it up to you.

**Misinterpretation / perception gap:**
> I understand the confusion — that section of the listing describes [X], which is [explain briefly]. I know it can be hard to tell from photos. If there's something specific I can help with, just say the word.

---

### Property Access / Lockbox Issues

**Guest can't get in.**

**Stance:** This is urgent — respond within minutes. Have backup codes and emergency contacts ready.

**Draft approach:**
> Oh no — let's get you in right now. The code is [CODE]. Make sure you're entering it on the keypad (not the touchscreen) and hitting the checkmark/unlock button after. If it's still not working, call/text me directly at [NUMBER] and I'll sort it out immediately.

## Refund and Compensation Requests

### The Core Rule
**Never volunteer a refund.** If the guest has a legitimate complaint and is asking for resolution, address the issue first. Only discuss compensation if: (1) the issue materially impacted their stay, (2) it was your fault or your property's fault, and (3) it wasn't fixed during the stay.

---

### Tiers of Response

**Tier 1 — Acknowledge Only** (minor inconvenience, fixable, no lasting impact)
> One sincere apology, fix it, move on. No financial offer.

**Tier 2 — Goodwill Gesture** (legitimate complaint, not severe)
> A small symbolic gesture: "I'd love to offer you a credit toward a future stay" or "I'm going to pass along a discount code for next time." This is not a refund.

**Tier 3 — Partial Refund Consideration** (significant impact: broken hot tub for 2+ nights, major cleanliness failure, listed amenity completely unavailable)
> Handle through Airbnb resolution center. Do not agree to a refund via chat — keep it on platform. Document the issue thoroughly before initiating.

**Tier 4 — Full Refund** (property uninhabitable: no heat in winter, flooding, major safety issue)
> Contact Airbnb immediately. Do not try to handle this alone.

---

### When a Guest Demands a Refund You Don't Think Is Warranted

**Guest says:** "I want a full refund or I'm disputing this with Airbnb."

**Draft approach:**
> I really do want to find a fair resolution here. I've looked into [the issue], and [brief honest assessment — e.g., "the hot tub was working when you checked in and our records show it was operational for most of your stay"]. I'm not able to offer a full refund in this case, but I do want to make things right. [Offer Tier 2 gesture if appropriate.] If you'd like to take this through Airbnb's resolution process, I completely understand — I'll respond there as well.

**Key notes:**
- Stay calm. An emotional or defensive response plays badly if Airbnb reviews the thread.
- Don't make accusations ("you broke it" / "you're lying") even if you suspect bad faith.
- Keep all responses factual and documented.

## Bad Review Threats

### The Core Rule
**Never negotiate over reviews.** Airbnb explicitly prohibits offering refunds in exchange for reviews (or withholding reviews as leverage). If a guest threatens a bad review, do not offer money to prevent it.

---

### Responding to "I'll leave a 1-star review"

**Draft approach:**
> I hear you, and I genuinely want to resolve whatever went wrong — that's always my priority. I want to be straightforward with you: I can't offer [refund/compensation] in exchange for a review outcome, but I do want to make this right on its own merits. [Address their actual complaint here.] If you feel the experience was below what was promised, I'd encourage you to share that honestly — and I'll respond honestly too.

**Notes:**
- Do NOT say "please don't leave a bad review" — this looks desperate and can be cited in Airbnb disputes.
- After a bad review is left, you get one public response. Use it professionally. Address the facts, don't argue with the guest, and signal to future guests what your standard is.

---

### Writing a Public Response to a Negative Review

**Structure:**
1. Thank them for their feedback (brief, genuine)
2. Acknowledge any legitimate point (1 sentence)
3. Provide factual context if the review is misleading (neutral tone, no drama)
4. Close with an invitation to return or a note about your commitment

**Example:**
> Thanks for the feedback. You're right that [issue] should have been caught before your arrival, and we've addressed it with our team. [If mischaracterized:] For context, [neutral facts]. We hold ourselves to a high standard and appreciate guests who help us get better.

## What to Always Document

When a guest reports a problem, before drafting any response:
- Note the time and date of the complaint
- Ask for (or note) photos if a physical issue
- Cross-reference the property's calendar for cleaning timestamps
- Check security camera logs if relevant (for property damage or rule violations)
- Keep all communication on-platform (Airbnb inbox or Hostfully) — never offer refunds via phone or text outside the platform
